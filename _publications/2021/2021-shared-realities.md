abstract: >-
  Recent advances in 3D reconstruction technologies allow people to capture and share experiences
  in three dimensions, yet little is known about how people perceive identity and privacy in such
  reconstructed experiences. We present ReliveReality, an experience-sharing method that uses
  deep learning-based computer vision techniques to reconstruct clothed humans, estimate 3D pose,
  and recover surrounding environments using only an RGB camera. ReliveReality can be embedded
  into social virtual environments, allowing others to socially relive experiences from multiple
  viewpoints on desktop or in VR. We conducted a 44-participant within-subject study comparing
  ReliveReality to traditional recorded video and to a version with avatar blurring obfuscation.
  Our findings reveal how people identify with reconstructed avatars, how obfuscation affects
  reliving experiences, and how privacy concerns influence sharing preferences. We discuss design
  implications for supporting identity, privacy, and control in reconstructed social experiences.
